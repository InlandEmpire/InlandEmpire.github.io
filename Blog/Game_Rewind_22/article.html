2022 - My Year in Gaming

Foreword

2022 was by far one of the most interesting years of my life. I graduated college, moved out of state, and completely changed careers in twelve short months. It was an extremely hard year for me, but just as rewarding. Despite all that was happening in my life, though, it proved to be a great year in gaming. I built a new gaming PC and completed a staggering 27 games and DLC. With the year over, I'd like to reflect on the titles I played in brief, tweet-like considerations on each. Rather than write exhaustive reviews, I'll briefly focus on the merits and demerits I personally found in each title in chronological order.

Rating System

I find number values to be inadequate to accurately describe one's opinions of a game, so using descriptors is my preference. I rate games based on a slightly-adapted version of Ross Scott's rating system, which he employs on his similar list of completed and uncompleted games. The system is as follows:

Fantastic: My life is better because I played these games. It doesn't mean the game has no problems, but the highs are so high I don't care about the lows.

Good/Great: Any game with this rating has my full seal of approval. I had no regrets playing this. Something about it kept it from being one of one of the best gaming experiences I've ever had, but that's not a mark against it.

Hazy: If you ask me what I thought of a game and I have to stop and think about it, then it gets this rating. It's got something going for it, but I beat the game and still don't know how I feel about it.

Love & Hate: These are games that would great if not for very serious issues. These games have extremely strong points underscored by very bad shortcomings.

Doubt: Games like this would probably be “Hazy,” but the longer I play them, the more I want them to be over. These are games that make me feel I was tricked into playing something hollow. These games may still be competent, but not good.

Pass: These are not necessarily bad games. In fact, many of these can be great games. What it means is it's the wrong game. These are games that just aren't for me, but were still finished.

Bad: These are games I played-through completely but I can in no way recommend. These games I probably didn't want to finish but still did out of some sense of resentment or pride.

Retired: These are games that have been retired from the queue due to a misalignment of taste or a shortfall of quality.

With that established, let's get to it!

Jan 27, 2022

Frostpunk (2018)

Platform: macOS

Time: 33:06

Rating: Fantastic

What a way to start the year. I originally was introduced to this game by Jacob Geller's video Fear of Cold. I'm a sucker for city-builders, especially those in unique situations and environments. When I first saw the concept of a steampunk-esque city kept barely alive through an icy apocalypse, I was immediately in love.
I won't beat around it, Frostpunk is hard— and it should be. Your objective is to grow your city through a pre-determined series of existential crises while unraveling a narrative about class struggle. How you approach each situation is largely up to the player, and you balance morality in the same way you balance your primary resources. Frostpunk keeps you on your frost-bitten toes, constantly making you think, “If we can sacrifice a little more, make due with a little less, give up just slightly more of our humanity, maybe we can make it.” I was hooked the entire campaign. Only after beating it did I learn that Frostpunk is actually made of multiple campaigns, each telling a new story in a different city's problems. That was an incredible treat to discover. Each campaign is just as harrowing as the next, constantly mixing up mechanics to keep you from being too good at any given start.
Frostpunk landed comfortably in my highest rating category for games, and I recommend it to any city-building enthusiast.

This War of Mine (2014)

Platform: macOS

Time: -----------------

Rating: Retired

Coming off the high of Frostpunk, I was incredibly disappointed by what's touted as 11-Bit Studios' previous masterwork, This War of Mine. This War of Mine is a game about what is easily Frostpunk's weakest element— morality.
Set in a nondescript war-torn city that's definitely not Sarjevo, This War of Mine is a game about gathering resources and keeping a small group of people alive through miserable conditions. I couldn't stand it. This War of Mine fails to add anything new to conversations about conflict. It's entire premise is, “War is bad,” which is easily one of the least controversial statements someone could make. On top of that, I couldn't help but feel it was incredibly insensitive to gamify a real struggle that far-too-many people experience every day. I'd say you might as well make a game about starvation and homelessness, but This War of Mine is already a game about that, too. I put it down after a few days.

Mar 10, 2022

Disco Elysium (2019)

Platform: macOS

Time: -----------------

Rating: Fantastic

When I first saw Disco Elysium talked about at The Game Awards in 2019, I thought it looked like a generic far-too-full-of-itself indie walking simulator created by egotist indie developers. I was almost right. Disco Elysium is, however, anything but generic. I first heard Disco Elysium described as, “A detective story at the end of the world,” an admittedly interesting premise. But that undersells it. Disco Elysium's end of the world, The Pale, is a geographic one. The story takes place in a fantastical radio-punk world separated from others by seas of un-reality. That world, though, is really only ever seen in the periphery. It's narrative is far more down-to-earth, almost hauntingly so.
Written by self-described communists living in a failed communist state, Disco Elysium takes place entirely within a city known as Martinaise that never truly recovered from a failed communist revolution and it's subsequent foreign occupation. Communism, fascism, and a handful of other in-universe ideologies are duking it out in the streets— the culmination of 10,000 years of struggle. But not as armies or faceless ideologies; as people, and as your own thoughts. Unlike any other game I've played, you build your character by strengthening which parts of your literal inner monologue speak the most. These voices constantly comment on one another, your actions, and the events of Disco Elysium's narrative. One of the best moments I've ever experienced in a game was after stepping out of Disco Elysium's starting area— the Whirling-In-Rags— into a rainy Martinaise morning and hearing Shivers richly describe the city in a profoundly poetic monologue. Disco Elysium is full of itself, yes, and it's developers have become infamously narcissistic, but Disco Elysium deserves to take itself seriously. It, and it's writers, have a lot to say worth hearing.
Not only do I consider Disco Elysium to be a phenomenal example of what video games can do to set themselves apart from other media, I consider it one of the most important pieces of literature this decade.

Mar 13, 2022

Hades (2020)

Platform: macOS

Time: 29:42

Rating: Fantastic

I've been a die-hard Supergiant fan since the launch of Transistor back in 2014. Everything they make is a masterpiece. The weird thing about the discussion surrounding this game, though, is that Hades seems to only be compared to Transistor and never the game that came between that and Hades, Pyre. Hades, to me, feels much more like an evolution of Pyre than any other of Supergiant's other titles. Honestly, Transistor is the least similar Supergiant game to Hades when you bring Bastion into the comparison. Normally I hate rogue-lights and games that require the player to frequently repeat the same content, but Hades is a special case. It's environments, music, and characters make ever play-through extremely engaging. Every run feels like a tangible accomplishment, especially as you start to crank up the difficulty. I'm excited for this one's sequel.

Mar 29, 2022

Final Fantasy XIV: A Realm Reborn (2013)

Platform: macOS

Time: 29:42

Rating: Fantastic

Yeah, we're at the fourth “fantastic” rated game on this list so far, but can you blame me? I started 2023 with banger after banger. The next ones won't all be rated as high, I promise. I've played a lot of MMOs in my day. My two most-played games ever are World of Warcraft and EVE Online. When I started college, my interest in WoW fell off a cliff, and I just didn't have enough time for EVE. I shouldn't have paid it much mind at the time, but the WoW exodus to FFXIV caught my eye, and I really wanted to try it. After Square Enix closed trial access while they upgraded their servers, I immediately created an account. Boy, am I glad I did.
FFXIV is one of the best MMOs I've ever played. Its story isn't incredibly engaging to start with, but once you get past the base vanilla campaign and the pre-heavenward stuff starts, it cranks up to 10. FFXIV excels in emergent gameplay and community, which most other theme-park style MMOs neglect. I love walking into an adventurer's guild and hearing a group of bards playing music or eavesdropping on conversations by the Limsa Lominsa aetherite.
It's class/job system is also incredibly unique, allowing players to switch between play styles at will. I want to play exclusively healers, so I'm excited to eventually graduate from White Mage and start trying other classes.
FFXIV's singular shortcoming is in world design. While its maps are huge and beautiful, FFXIV never lets the player slow down and enjoy them for long periods. The player is constantly moving from zone to zone, sometimes crossing Eorzea twice in a single quest. Because of that, it's hard for the player to grow an attachment to the world itself. That attachment is important in an MMO, and it's the main reason I love both World of Warcraft and EVE Online so much. Maybe once I reach max level and start leveling other classes I'll finally get to take my time in each zone like I want.

Apr 13, 2022

Plants vs. Zombies (2013)

Platform: iPadOS

Time: N/A

Rating: N/A (Replay)

Since this is actually the second time I've played this game, it doesn't get a formal rating score. The fact that I replayed it, though, should be a good indicator of my opinion on it. I first played Plants vs. Zombies as a kid on my first phone, an iPhone 4, so it felt right to play it again on a mobile device. Most of my time playing this game was on my iPad Pro between classes at school.
Plants vs. Zombies has aged incredibly gracefully. The one thing that I noticed the second time around, though, was that the game is ridiculously easy. Once you figure out one extremely basic plant formation, you can employ it on nearly every level to great success. I only ever had to restart a level once, and it was one of the wildcard arcade levels where you get random plants. That's a relatively minor complaint, though. I still think this is a must-play classic.

May 23, 2022

Dead Space (2008)

Platform: Windows

Time: 13:24

Rating: Good/Great

I graduated from college in May of 2022. As a reward for five years of grueling work, I built myself an awesome gaming PC intended to play most games at max settings, in 4K, at 120 FPS. The first game I chose to break this computer in on was… a game from 2008. I'm honestly not sure why I made that choice. I guess I just felt it was time to tackle this classic.Dead Space, like another zombie game mentioned above, has aged incredibly well. It's actually aged so well I'm surprised (and a bit frustrated) a remake was announced almost immediately after I completed it. Its visuals are cohesive enough that you don't really notice it's lack of visual fidelity, and its atmosphere and sound design draws the Ishimura together into a disturbingly immersive environment. I haven't yet played Dead Space's remake, but if it's even half as good the original, I'll need to. Great game, all around.

May 26, 2022

Frostpunk: The Last Autumn (2020)

Platform: macOS

Time: 5:36

Rating: Good/Great

Frostpunk's first major expansion, The Last Autumn, is set in the months leading up to the winter that sets the events of the base game in motion. It's an interesting and subversive spin on the game's formula, which still has a lot of mileage left in it.
I had fun with The Last Autumn, but overall felt it had a less compelling narrative and more frustrating series of objectives than the base game. The pressure put on the player in it's story is artificial, which makes it annoying to lose to. Instead of fighting against the forces of nature, the player is constantly struggling against incompetent bureaucracy and seemingly arbitrary deadlines set by an overseas administration. Fail to meet any of these deadlines and it's game over. It doesn't matter how well you're doing by any other measure, or if you're only a couple hours behind, you're met with instant loss. Because you aren't surviving a massive catastrophe, the end also doesn't quite hit the same. It's just kind of over when it's over. Still worth playing for any Frostpunk fan, though.

May 27, 2022

Dead Space 2 (2011)

Platform: Windows

Time: 9:59

Rating: Good/Great

Dead Space 2 was, naturally, the second game I played on my new rig. There's little I can add to the public conversation surrounding Dead Space 2 that hasn't already been said. It's great, but in different ways than the original Dead Space. The story feels like a natural progression, and being set on a giant, futuristic space habitat is extremely cool. Dead Space 2 is best known for being heavier on action than on horror. I don't think this is inherently a bad thing. I'm someone who appreciates developers experimenting with their titles rather than falling into a cycle of making the same game over and over. Though you might not scream and cry like the original Dead Space encourages, it's still sitting at a 94% positive rating on Steam for a reason. Give it a shot.

May 28, 2022

Frostpunk: On the Edge (2020)

Platform: macOS

Time: 2:51

Rating: Good/Great

Frostpunk's other main expansion is significantly smaller than The Last Autumn, clocking in at about half the length. Unlike any of the game's other scenarios, On the Edge is a direct followup to the base-game's main story. It's about as experimental as any of the other scenarios in the base-game, this time adding a settlement trading feature. You also don't have a generator to keep your village warm, though it doesn't get nearly as cold as you're used to dealing with by this point. In all, it's more of the same, which is in no way a bad thing. If you love Frostpunk like I do, you'll naturally love this expansion.

Jun 26, 2022

Fire Emblem: Shadows of Valentia (2017)

Platform: 3DS

Time: 37:28

Rating: Love & Hate

I started this game in an odd period of my life where I had just moved to another state for my first post-college job. Since the tiny mining town we had relocated to had no available housing for my then-fiancé and I for nearly a month after arriving, we were stuck in a one-bedroom AirBNB with the entirety of our possessions, still in their boxes, crammed anywhere we could fit them. All forms of entertainment were inaccessible to me except for my 3DS.
I'm a pretty big Fire Emblem fan. I've been slowly trying to get through every post-SNES title, which has been a monumental undertaking. After completing a number of GBA and Gamecube Fire Emblem titles, however, returning to the more-contemporary 3DS family of games was a breath of fresh air. Shadows of Valentia has a beautiful art style and incredibly compelling story, coupled with a handful of frustrating new weak points in the franchise's otherwise highly polished gameplay loop.
Unlike any other Fire Emblem title at the time, Shadows of Valentia requires the player to undergo sequences of dungeon crawling to obtain items, experience certain dialogue, and promote units. If there's one thing players buy Fire Emblem for, it is absolutely not dungeon crawling. Even worse, this is one of the Fire Emblem titles where you can hard-block your progression as you near the final boss by inadvertently under-leveling your characters or leveling the wrong ones. The 3DS Fire Emblem games have a serious problem with that.

Jul 9, 2022

Halo: Infinite (2021)

Platform: Windows

Time: 11:18

Rating: Bad

This is the most painful entry for me on this list. Those that know me know that I'm a massive Halo fan. I've read dozens of it's novels, played every game, including its obscure mobile spin-offs, and have long been a staunch defender of 343i. To this day I'm the only person I've ever met who genuinely loved Halo 5. When it was announced that Infinite was going to be an open world game, I was skeptical but hopeful 343i could pull it off.
Unfortunately, Halo: Infinite ended up being the first piece of Halo media I genuinely didn't enjoy. It's a complete mess from start to finish. Not only does it double down on all the mistakes Halo fans criticized previous games for most, it's a confusing jumble of conflicting design philosophy and narrative direction. The subsequent internal buckling of 343i says a lot about fan response to this title. The series really should have ended gracefully years ago.
